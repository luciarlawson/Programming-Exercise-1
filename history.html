A Brief History of North Berwick

North Berwick is a historic seaside town in East Lothian, Scotland, with roots stretching back to ancient times. Archaeological evidence suggests early settlements in the area, and by the 12th century, the town had become an important religious center with the establishment of North Berwick Priory. Its harbor also played a key role in ferrying pilgrims to St Andrews.

For centuries, North Berwick remained a small fishing and farming community, but this changed in the 19th century with the arrival of the railway. The town became a popular Victorian seaside resort, known for its scenic coastline, golf courses, and beaches. Today, it is a thriving tourist destination, famous for its links golf, the Scottish Seabird Centre, and the nearby Bass Rock, home to one of the world’s largest gannet colonies.

The North Berwick Witch Trials were one of Scotland’s most infamous witch hunts, taking place between 1590 and 1592. The trials were sparked by King James VI’s growing obsession with witchcraft, particularly after a storm disrupted his voyage from Denmark, which he believed was caused by witches.

Dozens of people, including Agnes Sampson, a respected midwife, and Dr. John Fian, a schoolmaster, were accused of gathering at North Berwick Kirk to perform dark magic against the king. Many were tortured into confessing and later executed, mainly by strangulation and burning at the stake.

These trials played a key role in Scotland’s wider witch-hunting frenzy. The North Berwick Witch Trials remain one of the earliest and most notorious witch hunts in Scottish history.
